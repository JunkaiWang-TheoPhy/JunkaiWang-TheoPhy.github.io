## SOP

### About Study

By the time I completed my undergraduate degree, I had gone through a series of fairly dramatic events. For that reason, I decided that before formally pursuing a PhD, I should spend some time observing how this era is changing. On the one hand, this would allow me to become more qualified before entering a more demanding stage of study and research. On the other hand, it would allow me to remain flexible in the face of a historical turning point, rather than locking myself too early into a path that is too narrow.

At first, I considered going to UCSD, seeking guidance from Professor Yizhuang You and continuing to study machine learning in physics under him. Later, however, the Yau Mathematical Sciences Center at Tsinghua University offered me a rare and excellent opportunity: I could remain in Beijing and continue studying and doing research there. I believed that spending time in Beijing, one of the country's most important scientific centers, would itself be meaningful. So I stayed.

I have always been someone who cares very little about titles. Wherever I am, and whatever stage I happen to be in, as long as I can keep moving forward, that is enough for me. In fact, in both scientific research and AI, I have grown faster than most PhD candidates. A great deal of this comes from the intensity of my enthusiasm, as well as from an environment that has been relatively open and genuinely encouraging of exploration. My co-advisors at Tsinghua gave me enough time and space to explore actively, make mistakes freely, and gradually build my own research judgment.

I often had deep discussions with Professor Luo Di at Tsinghua University. He is an expert at the intersection of quantum science and AI, and those conversations made me see more clearly that first-rate research today does not have to remain confined within the traditional academic system. Industry can also produce genuinely important work. I have always believed that big tech can do what big tech does best, academia can do what academia does best, and industry has its own distinctive value. In the age of AI especially, the sheer scale of resources available in industry has become impossible to ignore. Some of my friends at Tsinghua have done first-rate AI research while interning at companies and using the resources available there. That reality further changed the way I understand where research can happen.

Over the past year and more, I have improved very quickly. That progress has not been limited to the accumulation of knowledge; it has also involved a widening of both my perspective and my mentality. On the one hand, I have continued to work seriously in fundamental physics and have already achieved some initial results. On the other hand, because my co-advisor works on quantum algorithms and quantum artificial intelligence, I have also begun to enter quantum information science in a more systematic way, and I have built an initial foundation there.

### About AI

My understanding of AI changed through three important moments.

The first stage began in 2023. During my summer research experience in California, I had already started making broad use of large models for translation and text processing. But even by early 2024, my understanding of them was still limited. At the time, I believed they would revolutionize every profession that depended on the written word, for example drafting, translation, editing, and polishing. Yet I still had not truly realized that they had the potential to reshape the way almost every industry works. Part of that slowness came from the fact that I was still, in many ways, a young student immersed in the academic system.

The truly profound shift happened mainly over the past eight months.

The first decisive moment came in August 2025. I was attending a theoretical physics summer school at the Hangzhou Institute for Advanced Study when GPT-5 was released. That was the first time I felt strongly that large models were no longer merely useful tools. They were approaching something like a new intellectual infrastructure.

The second came in November 2025, at the Turing Forum jointly organized by three universities through Tsinghua's Institute for Interdisciplinary Information Sciences. I spoke with several professors in quantum science from Peking University and Shanghai Jiao Tong University about the latest progress in AI. They told me that GPT Pro could, within tens of minutes, generate remarkably strong responses to papers they had just completed, just submitted, or had even only recently seen accepted, and could also suggest genuinely useful directions for improvement. That was the moment when I truly understood that large models no longer possessed only textbook-level reasoning, but something approaching research-level reasoning. From that point on, I began integrating GPT systematically into my workflow, using it to improve research efficiency, accelerate idea iteration, and increase my rate of scientific production. At the same time, I began using Codex to explore the potential of vibe coding, from drafting papers to generating code to editing web pages, gradually building a new way of working.

The third came in February 2026, this time driven by OpenClaw. Frankly speaking, in my view OpenClaw itself was not a product that was already mature in some deeply satisfying way. But what it represented was extremely important. It marked the real beginning of an era: the maturation of foundation models, the emergence and systematization of the concept of skills, the formation of harness architecture, and the genuine opening of the command line to AI. In a sense, OpenClaw did not become influential merely because it was perfect, but because it truly connected the command-line world with AI agents. It pushed the AI ecosystem through one of the final steps toward mass accessibility.

I have always been someone who tends to fill my time. During the 2026 winter break, there were no teachers, classmates, seminars, or conferences around me, and I did not want to stop moving. So I began attending activities and gatherings in Peking University and elsewhere in society related to OpenClaw, learning the relevant knowledge more systematically, entering the community, and beginning to raise what people jokingly call "shrimp." At that time, I had not yet realized just how hot this direction would become after the new semester. But behind that rapid rise in temperature, I saw a new form of vitality and possibility. So I decided to truly commit myself to it, and within only a month I had already achieved results that were quite concrete.

For a long time, I believed that human teaching and human transmission were irreplaceable. That is also why, in the past, I was so committed to continually sharing my views on Zhihu: I believed that the transmission of knowledge had an irreducibly human dimension. But now my view has changed. I increasingly feel that good questions matter more than ready-made answers. I also find myself repeatedly haunted by another thought: if someone shuts himself in a study for five years, what kind of world will he find when he comes back out? Every scientific revolution and every technological revolution has its own finite window, and once that window is missed, the same opportunity may never return.

Some long and serious conversations I had with Cai deepened this recognition further. At least in many high-level judgments, the existing educational model has in some sense already failed. We are entering an era in which learning itself no longer carries much of a barrier. In such an era, what becomes genuinely scarce is the ability to do things: the ability to enter the field, organize resources, formulate questions, and push reality forward.

So this month, and these past six months, have changed me very deeply. This brings me back to my original theme. Since childhood, my dream has been to contribute to humanity. But the reality is that the world of fundamental science has become so difficult that, in many cases, even simple survival within it has become hard.

I often ask myself: you have long believed that formal science represented the best meeting point between personal vocation and collective need, but is that still true? With the arrival of the AI era, I have for the first time truly seen a better sweet spot. It carries broad social attention and rich resources. It can support real survival, and it may also genuinely change society. At the same time, it still preserves an intellectual frontier that is extraordinarily wide, extraordinarily primitive, and extraordinarily worth exploring. Today's AI, in a certain sense, still resembles the era of phlogiston theory. We still need enormous amounts of time and effort to discover its true physics and to wait for its own Einstein moment.

Since shifting toward AI, I have had new progress and new discoveries almost every day. I remain in a state of being constantly overwhelmed, yet constantly exhilarated by learning. That experience is very different from the way I previously studied, worked, and lived within the community of fundamental theory. For the first time, it has allowed me to feel directly that an era is opening, and that I am present within it.

Of course, if the right opportunity emerges in the future, I would still be willing to return to the broader scientific enterprise. But as the old saying goes: cultivate oneself, regulate the family, govern the state, bring peace to the world. If I truly want to move the world, I must first make myself wiser and stronger.

### About Entrepreneurship

Because the current investment environment in China is not especially favorable, my co-founder and I decided, after discussion, to move the project forward in the short term by entering various competitions. This choice had several practical advantages. It could help us win prize money and partially substitute for fundraising; it could increase exposure; it could bring us into contact with potential collaborators; and it could force rapid product iteration.

The results came even faster than we expected. In only one month, really only one month, we had already received more than 100,000 RMB in prize money, found reliable hardware engineers and collaborators, gained substantial media exposure, and received some investment interest. Frankly, even I found the speed of that transformation surprising. In almost exactly one month, I moved from being a fairly typical academically oriented student to becoming someone with a modest name in the young tech entrepreneurship circle. The feeling was profoundly surreal.

For a young student who has only recently stepped out of the academic system, one of the most important steps in entrepreneurship is finding trustworthy collaborators. In this respect, I have been fortunate. My collaborator is a full-time entrepreneur close to me in age. He has a strong humanities temperament, while I have relatively solid scientific and technical training. That complementarity in personality and capability allowed us to go very far in a very short time. He can deliver highly convincing pitches to investors and judges, and he can also use his entrepreneurial experience to help build the team and sustain morale. I often tell him, "Rick, if you need anything on the technical side, I can build it." Precisely because of that, our two-person team has gone from one success to another in the space of a single month.

Now, through the network we built through competitions, we have already found excellent hardware partners and distribution channels, and we plan to begin pilot hardware production this summer.

Compared with many other entrepreneurs, one of my strengths may come precisely from everything I went through in the world of basic mathematics and theoretical science. That is a domain that demands long-term commitment while offering very little immediate reward. I spent half of my youth on a cold bench, but in doing so I also trained the ability to work hard, concentrate deeply, and produce steadily. Many times I am surprised to find that my level of diligence is not necessarily greater than it once was, but in an entrepreneurial environment it is already seen by many people as an unusually intense level of commitment. It is exactly that long-trained reservoir of energy, that habit of valuing time, and that capacity for high-density learning of new material that allows me to keep updating and iterating myself in a rapidly changing era.

What we want to build in this venture is an Apple for the age of AI. We believe that every technological generation produces its own interaction gateway, and so AI-native interaction interfaces will be especially important. Strictly speaking, however, my ambition does not end with consumer products themselves. If the opportunity arises, I still hope to participate in the deeper scientific enterprise of the AI era. And your company offers me precisely such a precious opportunity.

### Why am I applying?

Your company represents a possibility that feels both unusual and genuinely rare to me.

First, the team is composed of well-educated researchers with physics backgrounds. That means that even if I am in industry, I can still remain in close and high-quality contact with the academic world. My scientific background also makes me feel naturally at home in such an environment. Broad and solid training in mathematics and physics is not merely part of my past; it remains the underlying way in which I understand the world, understand problems, and understand technology.

More importantly, this may be the closest opportunity I have, in the near term, to truly change the world. For me, that opportunity is not only about resources, and not only about career trajectory. It also has intellectual meaning. It means that I can bring together the habits of mind I formed in fundamental science, the sensitivity I developed in the wave of AI, and the capacity for action I have gained through entrepreneurship and product practice, and gather all of them into a larger direction.

That is why I hope to join your company.
